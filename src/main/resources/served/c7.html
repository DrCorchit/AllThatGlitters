Chapter 7

How does my character use magic?

Any sensible treatise on magic will disclose the following insight: magic is energy. Therefore, magic is classified according to whence this energy originates, the techniques used to manipulate it, and finally according to the overall purpose of its application. In fact, all spells follow the same basic pattern, whereby the magus identifies a source of energy, attains control, and finally disposes the energy according to his whim.

— Albrecht Riftenvorg, A Primer on Magic

In gameplay terms, players practice magic by learning spells and then casting them.

Learning Spells

In order to learn a spell, the player must either find spellbook or a trainer who has knowledge of that specific spell. Scholars partially circumvent this requirement by having access to a magical academy full of arcane tomes and professors. However, respectable institutions do not carry knowledge of unethical forms of magic such as necromancy or demonolatry (at least, not if they want to retain their reputation). Therefore, some forms of magic are inherently esoteric and difficult to learn, let alone master.

Once an appropriate trainer or tome has been located, the player must meet certain requirements, including:

  • A certain level of an attribute
  • Proficiency in certain skills
  • Prerequisite spells
  • A certain number of training slots

Once all these requirements are met, the player may learn the spell from the tome or trainer. Once learned, the spell can only be unlearned if the player intentionally forgets it, usually in order to free up training slots.

Casting Spells

Once a spell has been learned, certain additional requirements must be met in order to cast the spell. These requirements depend on the type of spell, which falls into one of four categories:

  • Burst Spells: Spells which are cast and take effect instantaneously or almost instantaneously.
  • Concentration Spells: Spells which are cast over a span of time. Usually require a burst of AP to initiate the spell and a smaller amount to maintain it.
  • Rituals: Spells that require careful and methodic preparation. These spells are not normally cast during combat.
  • Ceremonies: Spells that require a certain number of participants. Such spells usually consume some AP from each participant, each contributing some AP towards the casting of the spell. These spells are not normally cast during of combat.

Despite being intensely studied, magic remains an abstruse and opaque subject. Among those who study it, there is no consensus as to why certain individuals wield its power so effortlessly, why certain spells are so difficult to reproduce, and why other spells work at all. This inconvenience doesn't stop scholars from doing the one thing they are truly good at, however: categorizing things.

At the most basic level, the three types of spells are divination, conjuration, and evocation. Divination magic is concerned with information, whether collecting, transmitting, or fabricating it. Conjuration deals with spirits, whether they are summoned, bound, or banished. Finally, evocation deals with the disposition of energy, applying it for creation, preservation, transformation, or destruction. Each of these types are further subdivided and studied under various magical schools. In theory, schools ending in -mancy indicate divination while those ending in -urgy indicate evocation. In practice, commonfolk tend not to appreciate this distinction and make quite a mess of things. For example, there was once a distinction between necromancy and necromurgy; the former was concerned with contacting deceased spirits, while the latter was concerned with raising their bodies. However, the latter term has become practically obsolete, leaving the term "necromancy" to describe any form of death-related magic, divination or otherwise.

Scholars categorize magic primarily according to its source. Part of the reason they do so is certain sources of magic are considered inherently unethical. In general, two types of magic are considered unethical: demonolatry (conjuration of demons), and necromancy (harvesting of souls, and raising of the dead). All else is fair game.

  • Viviurgy, the working of the lifeforce

    Viviurgy is the most basic form of magic. Even when not used directly, it is often used to initiate spells from other schools. The most common variation of viviurgy involves a mage channeling his or her own life force in a practice called egomancy. This form of magic is extremely versatile because the entire process is self-contained. The downside of this practice is that its power is limited and overuse leads to exhaustion (and in rare cases, death) of the practitioner. Another downside is that it is difficult to use for divination, since it reaches inward rather than outward.

    In addition to auturgy, viviurgy also encompasses pair magic and ceremonial magic. In pair magic, one mage essentially becomes a living battery, acting as a source for the other's magic. In ceremonial magic, an assembly of participants works their magic in concert, harmonizing their power towards a shared goal.

  • Conjuration, the summoning or consultation of spirits

    Conjuration magic is categorized according to the type of spirit summoned, and the method used to summon. The upside of this form of magic is that the summoned spirits tend to wield greater power than a human can on their own. Most spirits rather enjoy being summoned, as the physical world is seen as fairly exciting and chaotic. Therefore, they usually make a reasonable attempt to fulfill the summoner's bidding, in hopes of being summoned again. However, having their own free will, spirits (especially fey) can behave unpredictably. In general, a supplication spell summons a spirit willingly, while a binding spell summons and restrains a spirit unwillingly. The latter practice is widely considered unethical.

    A proper discussion of conjuration requires knowledge of the four types of greater spirits: angels, demons, fey, and dragons, corresponding to the elements of air, earth, water, and fire, and the compass directions of north, south, east, and west, respectively. Thus, the art of conjuration is categorized into draconolatry, demonolatry, fey worship, and supplication.

  • Necromancy, the magic of the dead.
