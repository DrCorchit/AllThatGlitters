How do I play the game?

All That Glitters, like any tabletop roleplaying system, has three parts: The players, the Dungeon Master, and the rulebook. Each part has a duty:

  • The players should understand the abilities of their characters, avoid cheating or metagaming, respect each other and the DM, and give the DM feedback so that problems don’t arise out of the blue.

  • The DM should create a compelling setting, characters, and story and introduce challenging combat encounters while avoiding favoritism and respecting player agency.

  • The rulebook should provide a framework where players can reason about the outcome of their actions and see their characters progressing in potency, and assist the DM in creating encounters that are both unique and appropriately difficult.

In order to play the game, you need a group of people who are willing to meet on a semi-regular basis for about 3-4 hours. You also need someone to run the game: the Dungeon Master. If you’re reading this and you don’t know who your DM is, it’s probably you. Be the DM you wish to see in the world.

Session Zero

Once you have a group of friends and a DM, you should have a session zero. Session zero doesn’t have to be in-person; it can be a few text messages exchanged over the course of a few days. What matters is that everyone voices their expectations and ensures that they are compatible with their group before the first session.

Here are some important questions to discuss:

  1. What type of game do you want to play?
    Does your group want a lot of combat, a lot of story, or a mix of both? Do they want a main quest with a primary villain, or a sandbox world with a lot sidequests? Are problems like bandits in the countryside or necromancer cults part of an oncoming apocalypse, or merely business-as-usual? Having a single epic main quest can be fun, but the constant urgency tends to reduce player freedom. On the other hand, sandbox campaigns may feel unfocused and purposeless.
  2. Can player characters permanently die?
    Discuss player character death and total party kill (TPK) expectations. Are either of these outcomes possible? On the one hand, it can be heartbreaking to part with a beloved character. On the other hand, the possibility of player death leads to some extremely high stakes. Having the story end on a TPK is a very sour note; I think it is better for circumstances to intervene. However, having the possibility of true defeat does increase the authenticity of the threats.
  3. How do your characters meet?
    A tavern meeting is tried-and-true, but there are other options. Perhaps they’ve been enlisted by a local lord, jarl, or ealdorman to pursue some quest. Or maybe they've recently escaped from prison together, in which case one wonders whether they were falsely convicted, or are true scoundrels. Another possibility is that they were abducted and have awakened inside a labyrinth or cultist lair. Also, are any of the party natives to the starting area, or are they all foreigners? Can the players choose any race they want from the standard races list, or does the DM wish to create custom races to match a specific setting?
  4. What is the tone of the story?
    Do you want the story to be comedic, epic, grimdark, or realistic? How comfortable is your group with fantasy violence, sexuality, or politics? Oftentimes racism and sexism can make unsavory characters more compelling, but they may make some people in the group uncomfortable. Likewise, excessive humor at the wrong moments can ruin heart pounding moments of tension or suffocating dread.
  5. Are there any other concerns?
    For example, is player-versus-player combat allowed? (Not recommended.) Is this everyone’s first time playing the game, or is this a group of experienced players? Do you want to start at level 1, or higher?

Once your group answers these questions, you’re ready to make your characters and start playing the game. Remember that most of these questions don’t have wrong answers—you can play whatever game you want to play.

The Game Cycle

The game cycles between three states: narration, questioning, and action. During narration, the DM describes a situation, scene, or setting. At some point, the DM introduces one or more hooks that invite player interaction. For example, a suspicious NPC, a quest board at a tavern, a hallway branching into several mysterious passages, or even a crime being comitted.

Questioning happens after narration is complete, but before player have decided how they wish to respond. Players are usually trying to visualize the scene, probe for clues or hooks, or ask questions pertinent to their planned actions.

Once questioning is complete, the game moves into the action phase as the players tell the DM what they do. Sometimes, the DM simply narrates the outcome of these actions, completing the cycle. Other times, the action phase continues as the DM initiates either an event roll or a combat sequence.

Event Rolls

An event roll happens when the DM determines that the outcome of some player's action depends on chance, usually related to the capabilities of their character. To perform an event roll, the DM first determines which aspect of the character will be tested, usually an attribute or a skill. An attribute check tests the raw abilities of a character, such as moving a heavy statue or sprinting quickly, while a skill check tests more applied abilities which may be improved through training, such as aiming a bow or haggling with a merchant.

The DM then asks the player to roll for that skill, while privately determining a difficulty threshold (DT) typically ranging from 1 to 20. In response, the player rolls a d20 and adds their associated attribute score modifier. If the sum is greater than or equal to the DT, the character’s action succeeds.

Note: By tradition, rolling a 1 on a d20 is called a critical fail and results in an unusually bad outcome. Conversely, rolling a 20 is called a natural 20 and usually results in instant success.

If an event roll should have more than one potential outcome, the DM can add additional DT. For example, if the player is attempting to treat a wound, they may be asked to roll for medicine. The DM may decide that passing DT 12 indicates that the player is able to fully clean the wound and stanch the bleeding. Below 12 but above 6, and the wound is treated but later develops an infection. In any case, the DM does not usually disclose what the difficulty threshold(s) are—only the outcome.

After the event roll is complete, the DM describes what has changed or transpired, returning to the narration phase.

Other Rules

When rolling dice:

  • Rolling with advantage means to roll a die twice and take the higher result.
  • Rolling with disadvantage means to roll a die twice and take the lower result.
  • Advantage and disadvantage each other cancel out.
  • Rolling the middle means to roll a die three times and take the middle result.
  • If a number comes up twice when rolling the middle, take the repeated number as the result.

When rounding numbers, always round down.

When rules conflict, specifics override general rules.

Attributes

The capabilities of player characters are determined by six attributes: Strength, Dexterity, Speed, Intelligence, Charisma, and Willpower. Attributes describe the raw physical and mental capabilities of a player. For a given character, each attribute has a value, typically from 0 to 20. In general, a score of 0 indicates a total absence of the attribute (as with someone who is dead or unconscious) while a score of 20 indicates a nearly superhuman level of aptitude. For humans, a score of ten indicates an average level of proficiency.

Each attribute has an associated hard cap and soft cap. A player can surpass the soft cap only through strenuous training, necessarily neglecting other attributes to do so. The hard cap cannot be surpassed through any amount of training, however. For humans, the soft cap for each ability is at 16, and the hard cap is at 20. Racial bonuses to any attribute increase both the soft and hard cap by the same amount. For example, since dwarves receive a +2 bonus to strength, they can later increase their strength to 22, surpassing the human limit of 20, because their racial bonus has increased both their hard and soft caps for that attribute. The effect also works in reverse: dwarves take a penalty to speed, and so cannot train their speed stat beyond 19.

Not every character needs to be well-rounded, but characters with unusual ability score distributions should feel somewhat unbalanced to play.

Note: In most situations, we do not use the attribute score directly, but the attribute score modifier (ASM). The ASM for an attribute is obtained by subtracting 10 from the attribute score. An average human with 10 for all attribute scores would thus have 0 for their attribute score modifiers.

Skills

Skills are related to attributes in that they are also used for event rolls. Every skill has an underlying supporting attribute, whose ASM is added to the roll as with an attribute check. In addition to this, players add a bonus from training called a proficiency bonus. The number rolled on the d20 plus the ASM plus the proficency bonus determine the final value for the event roll.

Although every skill depends on at least one attribute, several skills have a secondary supporting attribute whose modifier may be used instead, under certain circumstances. For example, a player who is sneaking may decide that they are too clumsy to move quietly and instead opt to sprint past the guard while his gaze is turned. In this case, the player is trying to use their ASM for speed instead of dexterity for the stealth check. Whether this type of substitution is used is situational. In fact, certain circumstances requirethat the alternative attribute score modifier is used, while others permit it, in which case it would only be used if the second attribute’s score is higher for that character.

Each of these labels indicate that the player has either undertaken training or has an especial weakness in that skill. Deficiencies can be overcome and expertise and mastery can be attained through training even after a character is made.

When players obtain proficiency bonuses to the same skill from multiple sources, they do not combine to advance one’s proficiency in that skill to the next level. The only exception to this rule is that proficiency bonuses from a player’s race and class can be combined, allowing a player to begin the game with expert level in that skill.

Race, Class, and Size

Every character belongs to a race, which provides certain starting bonuses and determines the limits of their attribute scores. Race cannot be changed after a character is created. Examples of races include humans, elves, and dwarves.

Hit Points

Hit points (HP) are a resource that cause the player to fall unconscious when depleted. Since the character cannot move or fight when unconscious, keeping HP from falling to zero is extremely important. There are two sets of rules that govern what happens when a player falls unconscious: permadeath and modern.

Under permadeath rules, once the player has been downed, they roll a d20 every time it is their turn in combat. Every roll of a 10 or higher is considered a save, while every roll of 9 or lower is considered a fail. If the character makes three saves, they become stable and do not need to make additional saves to stay alive. However, if a character accumulates three fails, they are dead and can only be revived by powerful resurrection magic. Under this system, players can be revived and resume fighting after being healed by any healing spell, regardless of how many saves they have failed.

Note: Under permadeath rules, rolling a natural 20 immediately causes the player to become stable.

The modern rules offer an alternative system where unconscious players do not need to make saving throws to stay alive. However, they also do not regain consciousness after being healed by healing magic. Instead, they regain consciousness 1d4 hours after combat is over.

In gameplay strategy, modern rules prioritize keeping the characters conscious, making single-target healing spells quite valuable. Permadeath rules favor mass healing spells that resurrect the character whenever they fall. You should think carefully about which system works best for you.

A character’s HP cannot exceed their maximum HP stat. Initially, maximum HP is equal to the strength attribute, but it increases over time as a character levels up. The precise amount will be explained in more detail in the section on character leveling. All missing HP is restored after a long rest, while only one-half is restored after a short rest.

Abilities and Ability Points

Ability points (AP) are similar to HP in that both are depleted during combat, recovered by resting, and have a maximum amount that increases as players level. AP are expended as the player uses powerful spells and abilities. When AP is fully depleted, the character is said to be exhausted. There is not an immediate penalty for this condition, other than not being able to use critically necessary abilities.

Like HP, all missing AP are restored after a long rest. Unlike HP, only one-third of missing attribute points are restored after a short rest. Maximum AP is affected by the willpower stat similarly to the way maximum HP is affected by the strength stat.

Abilities are learned or innate skills that confer some benefit to the player, such as spells, combat maneuvers, or proficiency improvements. Abilities are categorized in a few ways. First, abilities acquired from a player’s class (such as the barbarian’s rage) are called innate abilities. Abilities learned from trainers are called trained abilities, and finally trained abilities which have been fully mastered are called rote abilities.

Abilities are also classified by whether or not they are explicitly invoked by the player or if they take effect automatically. The former are called active abilities and usually cost AP to activate, while the latter are called passive abilities (or perks) and take effect without the use of AP.

An example of a passive ability would be a perk that restores HP at the start of the player’s turn. Since the player is not required to do anything to receive this benefit, it is passive. On the other hand, an ability that allows the player to spend AP to deal extra damage would be an active ability.

Mitigation

Mitigation is a derived statistic which determines how likely a player is to successfully dodge or block an attack, similar to armor class from DnD. During combat, melee and ranged attacks are treated as an event roll testing the martial arts or accuracy skill, respectively. In this situation, mitigation provides the difficulty threshold for whether the attack succeeds. Thus, players with high mitigation are hit less often and therefore survive longer.

Mitigation is composed primarily of two values: dodge chance (DC), conferred by the player’s dexterity attribute, and block chance (BC), conferred by the player’s shields, armor and defense skill profiency. A third value, parry chance, is less common but used when a player is actively attempting to parry an attack. The three values are added together to calculate total mitigation, but certain attacks may bypass some or all of the three values, reducing effective mitigation against that tactic. For example, most types of armor are not ideal for absorbing blows from blunt weapons. Accordingly, bludgeoning damage automatically bypasses half of the target’s BC.

The player’s DC is normally equal to their dexterity attribute, while BC and parry chance start at zero. Block chance is increased by wearing armor and equipping shields, while parry chance is increased by taking the parry action while wielding a melee weapon.

How does my character fight?

Combat is pure chaos. It's nothing like the movies; you won't find any choreographed fencing with somersaults and pirouettes, dramatic music, or witty banter. Real combat is full of noise and blood and grime, broken bones, and the screams of the dying—or the eerie silence of the dead. Be that as it may, mortal combat is actually the pinnacle of problem-solving on a great many worlds. So you'd better get good at it.

In order to make sense out of chaos, combat is divided into turns and rounds. A turn of combat captures a single fighter doing their best to kill and not be killed for a single 5-second snapshot of time. Meanwhile, a round is completed when all combatants have completed their turn. Turns do not happen simultaneously, but in order of initiative, which will be explained shortly. Once combat has started, it continues through as many rounds as it needs to until only one faction remains, and all others have either surrendered, fled, or been slain. Then it ends, as quickly as it began.

    Actions

    At the beginning of every combat turn, players gain one main hand action, one offhand action, and movement points equal to one half their speed attribute. The various actions taken during the turn deplete these resources until the player has no more abilities to use, at which point their turn ends. Monsters may have more or fewer actions that human players, but otherwise their combat turns proceed in essentially the same way.

    In addition to these, combatants possess hit points and AP. Hit points are related to the strength stat, and the character or monster falls unconscious when they are reduced to zero. AP are related to the willpower stat, and are expended as the player or monster uses special abilities. When a character has zero AP, they are said to be exhausted, but there is not usually a penalty for this condition.

    Attacking

    The actions most commonly taken during a turn are those that deal damage. Attacks are handled in two parts, the first being an attack roll to determine whether the attack connects, and the second being a damage roll to determine how much damage is dealt. The attack roll is an ordinary skill check for either martial arts (for melee) or accuracy (for ranged). The DT of the attack roll is the mitigation stat of the target.

    To determine the damage of an attack which has connected, the player rolls that weapon’s damage dice and adds the ability score modifier to the total value. Each weapon has a unique damage die and one or more attack attributes. For example, a dagger’s damage die is a d4 and its attribute is dexterity. A player with a dexterity score of 12 rolling a 4 on a 1d4 would deal a total of 6 damage—4 from the weapon die, and 2 from their dexterity ASM. To give another example, a pike has a damage die of d12 and either speed or strength may be used as its attribute. A character with 14 speed rolling an 8 on the d12 would deal a total of 12 damage with their pike.

    Attack rules for spells are different and depend on the precise spell in question. Many spells partially or completely bypass ordinary mitigation or hit an entire area at once. In such cases, the target usually tries to pass a skill check to avoid full damage, rather than the caster trying to pass an ability check to cast the spell.

    Moving

    When moving around on a rectangular battle grid, the player expends 1 movement point for every vertical, horizontal, or diagonal move. Optionally, players can agree that diagonal movement costs 1.5 movement points. This effectively makes reachable tiles more circular and makes fractional movement points more relevant, but also increases the complexity of combat. In any case, a character cannot enter a tile unless they have the required amount of movement points remaining. For example, a character with 5.5 movement points would be able to move at most 5 tiles horizontally or vertically.

    In a 5-second combat round with 5-foot square tiles, a speed of 10 (average) translates to 5 feet per second, or around 3.5 miles per hours. This implies a typical jog speed of 7 miles per hour, and a sprint speed of 14 miles per hours.

    Grappling

    A player can attempt to grapple an enemy by using both a main and offhand action. If they are able to pass a martial arts check with a DT of enemy’s strength attribute, the grapple is successful. In this event, the grappled creature cannot move or take main or offhand actions during their turn. However, they can roll a martial arts check with a DT equal to the player’s strength attribute. If they succeed, they are no longer grappled and will be able to move and act normally on their next turn.

    Note that relative size affects grappling chances. If a player attempts to grapple a smaller enemy, they gain advantage on the martial arts check, while if they attempt to grapple a larger enemy, they gain disadvantage. No character may grapple a character more than one size class larger than they are.

    Reacting

    Reactions are special moves that happen in response to specific triggers, such as an enemy moving into or out of melee range. Reactions take effect before a player’s next turn, and give the player the option to expend actions for their pending turn to make special moves. Each player may take only one reaction per turn, and using a reaction expends some of the actions that would normally be available on their next turn.

    For example, if a player is engaged in melee combat with a monster that chooses to run away, the player can make a special move called an opportunity attack to punish the monster for turning its back on an attacker. This allows the player to deal the damage of an full attack while only expending an offhand action. However, on their next turn they will only have a main hand action, since their offhand action was used to damage the escaping monster. If that player decides to consume a potion on their next turn, they would have to expend a main hand action to do so, instead of the usual offhand action.

    Most monsters are also capable of scoring opportunity attacks against players, but players can use the disengage action to avoid this. Disengage allows the player to move out of melee range without triggering the opportunity attack reaction, useful for repositioning.

    Damage Types

    Smashing, slashing, poisoning, burning… There are quite a few ways to do someone in. Here's a list of damage types and their unique effects:

    Common damage types:

    • Slashing: Liable to cause bleeding.
    • Bludgeoning: More effective versus plate armors.
    • Piercing: More effective versus mail armors.
    • Fire: Liable to cause burning.
    • Freezing: Reduces and dexterity.
    • Lightning: Ignores metal armor.

    Rare damage types:

    • Acid/Corrosive: Causes armor damage which must later be repaired at a smithy.
    • Light/Holy: Causes extra damage to undead and evil aligned creatures.
    • Dark/Profane: Causes extra damage to good-aligned creatures.

    Good-aligned characters possess resistance to holy damage, and evil-aligned characters possess resistance to profane damage.

    Explosions usually deal a mix of damage types, usually bludgeoning and a magical damage types.

    Note: poison is not a damage type. Poison attacks deal unique effects depending on their type. Those that damage the victim over time inflict acid damage (but do not damage armor).

    How does my character become stronger?

    In All That Glitters, there are three ways that characters increase their power level: leveling, training, and looting. The main difference between these systems is that leveling provides guaranteed and predictable bonuses, while looting provides more irregular and chaotic bonuses that players cannot foresee. The bonuses from leveling promote planning and ambition, while the bonuses from looting are unique to each adventure, and promote character identity. Contrasting these, training allows players to take control of their own destinies and acquire the abilities they deem most useful.

    Leveling

    Each character’s skill as an adventurer is measured by their level, which typically begins at 1 and progresses up to 20. Typically, all characters in a party level up together, usually after a huge battle or major obstacle is overcome as the DM recognizes the adventuring party’s increasing skill and renown. This is known as milestone leveling. When leveling, players gain two types of bonuses: attribute training and class features.

    As a result of training, players may increase two of their attribute scores by one, if the increase does not put either attribute above the soft cap for that character. If the player wishes to increase an ability beyond the soft cap, they may only increase one attribute score.

    As part of attribute training, players increase their maximum HP and AP according to their strength and willpower scores, according to the following chart:

    Attribute Level HP or AP per level
    1-3 1
    4-5 1+1d4
    6-7 2+1d4
    8-9 2+1d6
    10-11 3+1d6
    12-13 3+1d8
    14-15 4+1d8
    16-17 4+1d10
    18-19 5+1d10
    20+ 5+1d12

    When rolling for max HP or AP, players should roll the middle. Meaning, roll three dice and take the middle result. This reduces the occurrence of extreme values, which can be frustrating at low levels.

    In addition to the attribute, HP, and AP bonuses, players gain unique bonuses (called class features) based upon their chosen combat class. These bonuses are discussed further in the next chapter, on creating a character.

    Training and Training Slots

    It is possible for characters to proactively learn many abilities from NPC trainers, such as skill proficiencies, combat tactics, and spells. In addition to any gold cost, learning abilities in this way costs training slots. Each player has training slots equal to their intelligence score, which represent the time and mental effort required to fully master the new skill. Most abilities consume four training slots at first, but that number is reduced by half every time the player levels up, rounded down. After leveling up, any trained abilities that consume only one training slot become rote abilities, and no longer consume training slots.

    If a player does not have enough training slots available to learn a new ability, they cannot learn the ability even if they have sufficient gold to purchase the training. In this case, players can choose to forget abilities they are in the process of learning. If this is done, the training slots for the forgotten ability are recovered but the gold use to purchase the ability is lost and the skill can no longer be used.

    In order to learn new abilities, players must first ensure that they meet the requirements of the ability they are trying to learn. For example:

    • Many abilities require a certain level of strength or intelligence, or other attribute.

    • Some abilities require a level of proficiency in a certain skill.

    • High level spells often require mastery of other low level spells before they can be learned.

    • Players must have sufficient training slots available for the ability they are trying to learn.

    Assuming they meet these requirements, players must find and hire a trainer of the appropriate discipline and skill level. After this, the new ability is gained, and can be used at any point in the adventure.

    Looting

    Looting refers to the acquisition of gold, weapons, armor, and magical items during the adventure. Importantly, looting—and in particular, the acquisition of gold—is considered a part of the leveling process itself rather than a mere footnote. Because gold is so closely connected with training, DMs usually provide players a certain amount of gold per level, as indicated in the following chart:

    Party Level Gold per player Typical Expenditures
    1 0-50 The party is focused on basic spell and ability training
    2-3 50-100 The party continues to train spells and abilities, while beginning to explore enchanted gadgets and consumables
    4-5 100-200 The party begins upgrading weapons and armor to improved metals such as titanium.
    6-7 200-300 As players reach the soft caps for their attributes, elite attribute training becomes useful.
    8-10 300-500 The party fully progresses beyond steel weaponry to mithril and adamant.
    11-15 500-1000 Players hone their skill sets and may pursue attribute training: Olympian.
    16+ 1000+ The party focuses primarily on the acquisition of rare and powerful trinkets and armor.

    Races

    I provide seven example races here, representing some of my favorite repeat offenders in the fantasy genre. That said, homebrewing new races is very much in the spirit of All that Glitters, and if a race you want to play isn’t in this list, you absolutely should create it. Alternatively, feel free to create your own lore for any of these races—don’t let my ideas stifle yours! Further on, I’ll explain some guidelines for creating new races (without upsetting gameplay balance).

    Without further ado:

    Human

    Overall, humans are proud, robust, and athletic. I daresay we are altogether quite handsome. Men average around 5’9” in height; women around 5’4”. Weight varies greatly, usually between 100-200 lbs. We live between 70 and 90 years, if we do not die in one of our many wars.

    Although we humans may seem an uninspiring choice to some, it’s all a matter of perspective. To dwarves, we are tall and regal. To elves, we are robust and warlike. Among saurids, we are called slow and loud. And so it goes.

    Human characters start with a single bonus point in an ability of their choice, and may take a level of proficiency in any skill.

    Elf

    Graceful and elegant, elves exemplify much of what humans aspire to be. In height, male elves average about 6’3”; female elves about 5’9”. Weight varies between 120-200 lbs. They are barely heavier than humans, despite their increased stature. The gods have truly blessed their race: they live around 200 years at least, and some (of the royal line) as long as five hundred.

    Over time, their kind has diverged into three bloodlines. Descendants of the royal bloodline are called Eldarii and excel in magic and all manner of letters. They incorporate magic into nearly every level of society. As a consequence, even their young children know basic spells. They are invariably blonde haired and fair skinned, and persistently arrogant. Some of them attempt to repent their hubris, but I have come to prefer the frank honesty of arrogance to a feigned humility.

    Members of the common line are called Numenarii, and pursue archery, hunting, fishing, and farming. They have interbred on occasion with humans, and so take on darker hair and skin tones. They imitate the arrogance of the Eldarii, though it is but a veneer: they are quite insecure.

    Members of the third bloodline are called Drow, and are descended from the line of the nameless usurper. Tales of their cruelty are exaggerated, but not by much. Their skin is bluish gray, and their hair silver. They do not spare themselves from their own ruthless designs, and many attempt to escape their subterranean holds. Those few who do often become brave adventurers.

    All elven characters suffer a -1 penalty to strength, and gain bonuses depending on their bloodline. Those of the royal line gain +2 INT and knowledge of a level 1 spell, plus proficiency in literacy, along with deficiency in survival. Members of the common line gain +2 DEX and proficiency in nature and accuracy. Drow gain +2 WILL and proficiency in perception and stealth. Drow also have the gift of darkvision.

    Dwarf

    A short and ruddy race. Male dwarves average around 5 feet in height, while their females average around 4’8”. Despite their stature, they weigh between 100-160 lbs. It is customary for their men to grow long beards, though beards longer than twenty inches are seldom seen outside storybooks. They live a bit longer than humans, so a century and a half is not out of the question.

    The dwarves of yore possessed insatiable bloodthirst and fought man, elf, and dragon alike in a great many wars. Times have changed, and they have been at peace nigh on two millennia. They are now greatly concerned with the exploration of mountains and caves, the industry of mining, and the acquisition of gold.

    Despite their unusual habits and short stature, dwarves are actually a fairly well balanced race that can excel in many roles. Dwarves gain +1 STR and +1 WILL at the cost of -2 SPD, and choose an additional +1 in STR or WILL. Also, dwarves have a keen sense for gold and have advantage on perception and persuasion checks when searching for treasure or bartering with merchants. All dwarves have proficiency in defense.

    Halfling

    The shortest of the races, halfling men average 3’9” in height. Their women are yet shorter, scantly 3’6” by average. A tall halfling may resemble a short dwarf, though the former will be discernible by his slender build and wispy facial hair, if he has any at all. Halflings weigh only 50-100 lbs, and I’ll wager a drink you can’t find a heavier one in under a fortnight. They are somewhat long lived, often lasting a bit more than a century.

    As timeless as their quaint and cozy country villages may seem, they are no strangers to change. Nearly half of them are now second or third generation immigrants in large cities. They are unwelcome there on account of their ill reputation: it is said that nearly a third of them are criminals. As thieves, they are exceedingly sly.

    Halflings tend to be unbalanced characters, having a -2 penalty to STR and a -2 penalty to SPD. They make up for this with a +2 to DEX, and another +2 to INT, CHA, or WILL. Urban halflings start with proficiency in parkour and sleight of hand, while rural halflings start with proficiency in nature and survival.

    Orc

    Stout and rippling with muscle, orcs are warriors to the last. Their menfolk are nearly as tall as the elves, around 6’, and their women are barely any shorter, around 5’10”. Fully grown, they weigh somewhere between 180 to 300 lbs—quite formidable! Their hair is almost invariably black or gray, while their skin ranges from dark green to pale yellow or gray. In years, they may reach 60 if they are lucky; usually less. They make up for this by living with what I will graciously call ‘extreme vigor’.

    The history of orcs is full of long and bloody intertribal conflicts. Despite this, they have made steady progress as a species and have transitioned from cave dwellings to small villages, then larger villages, until now many think to mingle with humans, dwarves, and halflings on the frontiers of civilization. (Elves will have none of their ilk.) They are well-suited to mercenary or guard work. They make poor thieves or wizards.

    Orcs gain +2 to STR and WILL, at the cost of -1 to DEX and -1 INT, and an addional -1 to either INT or CHA. They have proficiency in intimidation and survival.

    Tiefling

    Of middling stature and musculature, tieflings are nearly human. Yet as the saying goes, ‘so close and yet so far.’ By virtue of their debauched bloodline (the devil-dealings of their ancestors being no small secret) they are cursed with horns, pointed ears, and skin of a blood-red hue (or other outrageous colors). Almost everywhere, they are shunned by all ‘good’ folk. Yet I think they can be accorded a little understanding, for they are neither warlike nor pompous, and wisely take their poor lot in life with a dose of good humor.

    It is difficult to give a straightforward account of their physical nature, because it is so widely varied. I have seen some as short as 5’ and others as tall as 6’8”. Their are neither particularly bulky nor slender, and so their weight is comparable to that of a human. Only in longevity have the gods relented, granting them perhaps 110 years, around the same span as a halfling.

    Tieflings typically live in colonies suitably removed from human and elven settlements. They have dealings with dwarves and halflings, who treat them fairly. They quietly pursue industry and keep to themselves, but their young oft grow weary of their meek ways and seek adventure and companionship abroad.

    Tieflings gain +1 to CHA and DEX, and an additional +1 to INT or CHA, at the cost of -2 WILL. They possess the gift of darkvision, and can see in total darkness to a range of 3 yards, or dim light to about 30 yards. They have proficiency in performance and either medicine or perception.

    Aarakocra

    Flighty and covered with feathers, the Aarakocra are an extremely vibrant and colorful race. There are as many sub-races of these as of man or elf; it seems that many take after some species of bird or another. So you have one with feathers of azure and gold, another crimson, cream, and jade, and a third black as a raven. In general, they are about the size of a man, but extraordinarily light. I think 5'6" is a typical height, but they weigh less than 80 pounds. Their longevity varies greatly, and some have a span of up to 250 years, rivaling even the elves. However, this is not common and I have met very few over the age of ninety or so.

    On my travels, I have observed that Aarakocra races and tribes can be roughly divided into two categories: raptors and songbirds. The former are taller and stronger, and possess extremely keen hearing and sight. As for the songbirds, they are extremely quick with their limbs and can mimic almost any sound they hear. They have a reputation for bing tricksters, and are in the habit of gathering shiny things, as their brothers the ravens and crows do.

    Aarakocra gain a +2 bonus to SPD, but -2 to STR. All aarakocra can glide and are immune to fall damage. Raptors gain +1 STR and proficiency in perception, while songbirds gain +1 DEX and proficiency in performance.

    Saurid

    Slinking, skulking, scurrying—these are words befitting a saurid. Treading the slender line between man and beast, they are a fierce folk. As much lizard as man, they possess scaly hides, long tails, and snouts lined with razor sharp teeth. Some say they are cruel, but I having observed them closely on more than one occasion, I would rather describe them as practical.

    A saurid is typically shorter than a man, but they grow all their lives until extreme old age. Some can become quite large. Their females are a bit taller and more muscular than their males. In terms of height, they reach about 5’6” while the male might reach only 5’2 or so. They are not long lived, and seventy years is a ripe age for a saurid.

    The saurids I have seen are fairly xenophobic, and live in colonies inside caves usually near water. They hunt, fish, and raise their hatchlings quietly, having few dealings with man or elf, except for the occasional territorial clash. They have developed a unique sort of architecture, and their colonies are quite remarkable, often lavishly decorated with precious stones and cut stone. Their predilection for gathering all things shiny has attracted dwarven traders, which has brought the common tongue to their knowledge. Many of them now seek adventure abroad, bringing with them their fearsome and curious ways.

    Saurids gain +2 to STR and SPD, at the cost of -2 INT and -1 CHA. They possess the gift of darkvision, able to see in near total darkness. They have deficiency in literacy, but may take proficiency in two out of initiative, stealth, or perception.

    When creating new races, consider following these general:

    1. No stat bonus should exceed +2, and no stat penalty should exceed -2.
    2. The sum of all racial bonuses minus all penalties should equal 1.
    3. Every race should have at least one proficiency bonus, but no more than 2.
    4. Ideally, the player should have some choices regarding either subraces, attributes, or proficiencies.
    5. Be careful making races that give +2 bonuses to both strength and dexterity, or both intelligence and will. Such races would make very strong warriors or wizards respectively with very little downside.

    Classes

    For a given character, their combat class answers the question how do I fight? and also suggests their background or origin story. For example, the barbarian fights with reckless melee attacks, showing little regard for personal safety or technique. Accordingly, a barbarian would be more likely to hail from a remote farming village or an uncivilized tribe than from a royal court or a monastery.

    There is some ongoing debate as to the ideal number of classes in a TTRPG system. Too few classes, and players are less likely to find one that really captures their interest and vision. Too many, and the player is at risk of being drowned by excess choices, which may not be well balanced, either. I’ve decided to use 20 classes, which are grouped into five categories. Players who find the 20 classes intimidating can thus choose between one of these five categories, and then between the four classes within that category. Hopefully it is easier to make two simple choices than one complicated choice.

    Alignment

    In keeping with traditional TTPRGs, we use a simple alignment system to roughly track a character’s motives. The alignment chart has two axes: lawfulness and morality. Lawfulness describes a character’s predictability and their faith (or lack of faith) in ideals. Meanwhile, morality describes a character’s level of empathy and compassion for others, or lack thereof.

    The Alignments

    A character’s alignment provides a rough guide to how they intend to behave. Of course, a character’s alignment can change over time, and not all actions are perfectly representative of their alignment. Also, certain actions don’t have a middle ground. You either run into a burning orphanage to save the children, or you don’t. Such circumstances can be polarizing, driving character development and pushing neutral characters towards the extremes of morality. Likewise, polarizing events might also serve to disillusion good characters and turn them evil or neutral, or inspire evil characters to reconsider their ways.

    Before we move on, I’d like to dispel some misconceptions regarding lawfulness and the alignment chart in general. First, alignment should always be secondary to a character’s personality. Second, lawfulness is often used to capture two separate ideas. The first interpretation is a tendency to follow laws while the second is a tendency to believe in and adhere to some fixed set of ideals. This leads to confusion because a seemingly lawful paladin could suddenly start breaking laws if they travel to a land where the foreign laws conflict heavily with their ideals. It’s not always clear whether this represents a change in alignment or not. Although there’s no single correct interpretation, I suggest a third interpretation that lawful characters tend to behave according to reason and logic, while chaotic characters behave according to emotion. One advantage of this interpretation is that it allows lawful characters the flexibility to sometimes be driven by pure emotion, and also explains when and why chaotic characters behave unpredictably—as opposed to just providing chaos for the sake of chaos.

    Weapons

    Certain weapons have special properties, which affect how they behave:

    • Piercing: The weapon deals piercing damage only.

    • Slashing: The weapon deals slashing damage only.

    • Bludgeoning: The weapon deals bludgeoning damage only.

    • Cut-and-Thrust: The item can deal either piercing or slashing damage.

    • Parrying: The weapon allows parrying, increasing parry chance for the weapon by the indicated amount.

    • Blocking: The weapon allows blocking, increasing BC by a certain amount, and double when actively blocking.

    • Mordhau: The weapon can be held in a reversed grip to change the damage type to bludgeoning. This changes the weapon’s damage die. (Larger swords have this property.)

    • Versatile: The weapon can be held in a two handed grip for extra damage.

    • Nimble: Dexterity can be used to augment the weapon’s damage, instead of strength.

    • Improvised: The item is not primarily intended as a weapon. The damage die is rolled with disadvantage.

    • Light: The weapon can be dual-wielded, alongside another light weapon. Of a ranged weapon, indicates that the weapon can be fire with one hand. (All small weapons have this property.)

    • Heavy: The weapon requires two hands to use effectively, and has a strength requirement. (All great weapons and polearms have this property.)

    • Sharp: The critical threshold for the weapon is reduced to the indicated amount. (Daggers and Stilettos have this property.)

    • Reach: The weapon can hit targets 10 feet away, instead of 5.

    • Throwable: In addition to regular use, the weapon can be effectively thrown.

    • Thrown: The weapon is onlythrown, and never retained in the hand.

    • Projectile: The weapon fires a specific type of projectile.

    • Loading: Once discharged, the weapon must be loaded again to be re-used. Loading such a weapon takes a full main hand action. The weapon’s damage is also not affected by any attributes of the bearer, such as strength or dexterity.

    Armor

    Many forms of armor are available for the prospective adventurer. In general, nimble and dexterous characters such as thieves and assassins prefer leather armor or brigandine. Barbarians, mercenaries, inquisitors, and most others usually make use of medium armors, such as breastplate or chainmail. Knights, veterans, and crusaders often wear splinted mail or even full plate, being stouter folk.

    Note: Cheaper forms of chainmail struggle versus piercing damage, but high quality mail no longer has this weakness. However, those talented with the estoc sword may yet be able to pierce it.
