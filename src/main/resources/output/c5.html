Combat is pure chaos. It's nothing like the movies; you won't find any choreographed fencing with somersaults and pirouettes, dramatic music, or witty banter. Real combat is full of noise and blood and grime, broken bones, and the screams of the dying—or the eerie silence of the dead. Be that as it may, mortal combat is actually the pinnacle of problem-solving on a great many worlds. So you'd better get good at it.

In order to make sense out of chaos, combat is divided into turns and rounds. A turn of combat captures a single fighter doing their best to kill and not be killed for a single 5-second snapshot of time. Meanwhile, a round is completed when all combatants have completed their turn. Turns do not happen simultaneously, but in order of initiative, which will be explained shortly. Once combat has started, it continues through as many rounds as it needs to until only one faction remains, and all others have either surrendered, fled, or been slain. Then it ends, as quickly as it began.

    Combat begins with a skill check, as both players and npc combatants roll for initiative. In All That Glitters, rolling for initiative is an ordinary event roll, and therefore all other rules such as proficiency bonuses apply. When two characters roll the same initiative, players move before monsters, and two players rolling the same initiative may either reach an agreement about who moves first or roll a d20 to decide. Monsters rolling the same initiative move in whatever order the DM decides.

    Optionally, the DM may decide to allow players to roll for recognition to see the stats of the monsters they are about to fight. This check depends on the survival skill and its DT is determined by the rarity of the monsters in question:

    Monster Rarity Interpretation Typical DT
    Common The monster's physicalities are thoroughly documented in literature, and its attacks are an (at least) annual occurance. 18
    Rare The monster has been seen before, but not studied thoroughly. It may have been years to decades since the last sighting. 24
    Legendary The monster has not been sighted in perhaps a century or more. Many doubt that it still exists, or ever existed. 30

    The recognition roll is normally made with disadvantage if the area is obscured by heavy fog or darkness. Conversely, players may roll with advantage if attempting to discern monsters that emit distinctive noises or smells. If successful, the players gain knowledge of the monster's HP, attacks, and mitigation stat, and any other information the DM chooses to reveal. If there are several monsters attacking together, the roll should be done in one go rather than rolling again for each unique type of monster in the encounter.

    After initiative and recognition are rolled, combatants begin to take turns in order of their initiative score. Combat then proceeds until the last monster has acted, completing the first round of combat. Subsequent rounds proceed in the same fashion, with characters and monsters taking turns attacking each other until only one faction remains, at which point combat ends.

    Note: Sometimes, players may have a chance to view their enemies from a distance before they are recognized. In this case, they may roll for recognition before combat begins, usually with advantage.

    During an ambush or surprise attack, sometimes one side is so completely discombobulated that they are unable to defend themselves decisively. This condition is called surprise and has the following effects:

    • Characters cannot make recognition rolls
    • Initiative rolls are made with disadvantage.
    • Reactions are unavailable until after the first turn.

    Note: Each combat round corresponds roughly to 5 seconds of time in the game world.

    Actions

    At the beginning of every combat turn, players gain one main hand action, one offhand action, and movement points equal to one half their speed attribute. The various actions taken during the turn deplete these resources until the player has no more abilities to use, at which point their turn ends. Monsters may have more or fewer actions that human players, but otherwise their combat turns proceed in essentially the same way.

    In addition to these, combatants possess hit points and AP. Hit points are related to the strength stat, and the character or monster falls unconscious when they are reduced to zero. AP are related to the willpower stat, and are expended as the player or monster uses special abilities. When a character has zero AP, they are said to be exhausted, but there is not usually a penalty for this condition.

    Here is a chart of actions available to players.

    Action Cost Effect
    Attack Main Hand Action The character attacks with their main-hand weapon, but not a two-handed grip or dual-wield attack.
    Double Attack Main and Offhand Action The character performs a two-handed grip or dual-wield attack.
    Offhand Attack Offhand Action The character attacks with an offhand weapon, such as a dagger strike or shield bash.
    Ability Ability Dependent The character uses a special ability.
    Throw Main or Offhand Action The character throws an object such as a spear. Throwing with the offhand deals no damage.
    Grapple Main and Offhand Action The character attempts to wrestle an enemy, rendering them incapable of taking actions or bonus actions.
    Block Off Hand Action The player uses their shield to block incoming attacks, doubling its BC.
    Parry Main Hand Action The player attempts to use their melee weapon to deflect incoming attacks.
    Disengage Off Hand Action The player disentangles themselves from adjacent melee attackers.
    Help Main Hand Action The player attempts to rouse an unconscious player, or assist another player with an assailant or condition.
    Item Offhand Action The player uses an item such as a potion. Some items can only be used with the main hand.
    Move Movement Points The player traverses the battlefield on foot.
    Jog Offhand Action The player doubles their remaining movement points.
    Sprint Main Hand Action The player quadruples their remaining movement points.
    Jump Offhand Action The player jumps across the battlefield, gaining height and bypassing obstacles.

    Attacking

    The actions most commonly taken during a turn are those that deal damage. Attacks are handled in two parts, the first being an attack roll to determine whether the attack connects, and the second being a damage roll to determine how much damage is dealt. The attack roll is an ordinary skill check for either martial arts (for melee) or accuracy (for ranged). The DT of the attack roll is the mitigation stat of the target.

    To determine the damage of an attack which has connected, the player rolls that weapon’s damage dice and adds the ability score modifier to the total value. Each weapon has a unique damage die and one or more attack attributes. For example, a dagger’s damage die is a d4 and its attribute is dexterity. A player with a dexterity score of 12 rolling a 4 on a 1d4 would deal a total of 6 damage—4 from the weapon die, and 2 from their dexterity ASM. To give another example, a pike has a damage die of d12 and either speed or strength may be used as its attribute. A character with 14 speed rolling an 8 on the d12 would deal a total of 12 damage with their pike.

    Attack rules for spells are different and depend on the precise spell in question. Many spells partially or completely bypass ordinary mitigation or hit an entire area at once. In such cases, the target usually tries to pass a skill check to avoid full damage, rather than the caster trying to pass an ability check to cast the spell.

    Moving

    When moving around on a rectangular battle grid, the player expends 1 movement point for every vertical, horizontal, or diagonal move. Optionally, players can agree that diagonal movement costs 1.5 movement points. This effectively makes reachable tiles more circular and makes fractional movement points more relevant, but also increases the complexity of combat. In any case, a character cannot enter a tile unless they have the required amount of movement points remaining. For example, a character with 5.5 movement points would be able to move at most 5 tiles horizontally or vertically.

    In a 5-second combat round with 5-foot square tiles, a speed of 10 (average) translates to 5 feet per second, or around 3.5 miles per hours. This implies a typical jog speed of 7 miles per hour, and a sprint speed of 14 miles per hours.

    Grappling

    A player can attempt to grapple an enemy by using both a main and offhand action. If they are able to pass a martial arts check with a DT of enemy’s strength attribute, the grapple is successful. In this event, the grappled creature cannot move or take main or offhand actions during their turn. However, they can roll a martial arts check with a DT equal to the player’s strength attribute. If they succeed, they are no longer grappled and will be able to move and act normally on their next turn.

    Note that relative size affects grappling chances. If a player attempts to grapple a smaller enemy, they gain advantage on the martial arts check, while if they attempt to grapple a larger enemy, they gain disadvantage. No character may grapple a character more than one size class larger than they are.

    Reacting

    Reactions are special moves that happen in response to specific triggers, such as an enemy moving into or out of melee range. Reactions take effect before a player’s next turn, and give the player the option to expend actions for their pending turn to make special moves. Each player may take only one reaction per turn, and using a reaction expends some of the actions that would normally be available on their next turn.

    For example, if a player is engaged in melee combat with a monster that chooses to run away, the player can make a special move called an opportunity attack to punish the monster for turning its back on an attacker. This allows the player to deal the damage of an full attack while only expending an offhand action. However, on their next turn they will only have a main hand action, since their offhand action was used to damage the escaping monster. If that player decides to consume a potion on their next turn, they would have to expend a main hand action to do so, instead of the usual offhand action.

    Most monsters are also capable of scoring opportunity attacks against players, but players can use the disengage action to avoid this. Disengage allows the player to move out of melee range without triggering the opportunity attack reaction, useful for repositioning.

    Defeat

    When a monster or player character loses all their hitpoints, they can no longer fight and fall unconscious. What happens to a player character at this point depends on whether you are playing according to permadeath or modern rules.

    Under permadeath rules, once the player has been downed they roll a d20 every time it is their turn in combat. Every roll of a 10 or higher is considered a save, while every roll of 9 or lower is considered a fail. If the character makes three saves, they become stable and do not need to make additional saves to stay alive. However, if a character accumulates three fails, they are dead and can only be revived by powerful resurrection magic. Under this system, players can be revived and resume fighting after being healed by any healing spell, as long as they have not actually died.

    Note: Under permadeath rules, rolling a natural 20 immediately causes the player to become stable.

    The modern rules offer an alternative system where unconscious players do not need to make saving throws to stay alive. However, they also do not regain consciousness after being healed by healing magic. Instead, they regain consciousness 1d4 hours after combat is over.

    In gameplay strategy, modern rules prioritize keeping the characters conscious, making single-target healing spells quite valuable. Permadeath rules favor mass healing spells that resurrect the character whenever they fall. You should think carefully about which system works best for you.

    Damage Types

    Smashing, slashing, poisoning, burning… There are quite a few ways to do someone in. Here's a list of damage types and their unique effects:

    Common damage types:

    • Slashing: Liable to cause bleeding.
    • Bludgeoning: +3 to hit versus plate armors.
    • Piercing: +3 to hit versus mail armors.
    • Fire: Liable to cause burning.
    • Freezing: Reduces target dexterity.
    • Lightning: +3 to hit vs metal armor.
    • Force: Liable to knock the target prone.

    Rare damage types:

    • Acid/Corrosive: Causes armor damage which must later be repaired at a smithy.
    • Light/Holy: Causes extra damage to undead and evil aligned creatures.
    • Dark/Profane: Causes extra damage to good-aligned creatures.

    Good-aligned characters possess resistance to holy damage, and evil-aligned characters possess resistance to profane damage.

    Explosions usually deal a mix of damage types, usually bludgeoning and a magical damage types.

    Note: poison is not a damage type. Poison attacks deal unique effects depending on their type. Those that damage the victim over time inflict acid damage (but do not damage armor).

    Status Effects

    During combat, sometimes characters fall prey to certain temporary conditions that reduce their effectiveness in combat.

    Name Effect Notes
    Bleeding Each turn, the target takes damage equal to the number of turns of bleeding remaining. Inflicted by slashing damage. May be alleviated by the "help" action.
    Grappled The target is incapacitated, but can roll for martial arts on its turn in order to break free. May be alleviated by the "help" action. The ally giving aid may roll for martial arts to break the target free.
    Frozen The target is encased in ice and incapacitated. They are resistant to all forms of damage except bludgeoning. Ends the "burning" and "bleeding" status effects.
    Incapacitated The target cannot move or take actions. Enemies have advantage on attacks against the target.
    Burning The target takes 4 fire damage each turn. At the start of its turn, the target must make a DT 12 willpower check or become incapacitated for the remainder of its turn. Alleviated by the ability Fire Mitigation Technique.
    Poisoned After a set number of turns, the poison takes effect. Alleviated by the antidote consumable.
    Petrified The player is incapacitated encased in stone
