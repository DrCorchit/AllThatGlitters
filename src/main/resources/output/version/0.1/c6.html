Although much of the action outside of combat is fairly open-ended, there are specific rules for certain actions:

  1. Resting
  2. Travel
  3. Swimming
  4. Sneaking
  5. Pickpocket
  6. Lockpicking
  7. Haggling

Resting

Over time, your characters become ragged and weary as they take damage and expend AP during combat. Although some HP and AP can be recovered with potions or abilities, most recovery is done through resting. There are two kinds of rests:

  • Long Rest: The entire party makes camp and sleeps for 8 hours, completing the adventuring day. If in the wilderness, this typically involves setting up camp, building a fire, cooking dinner, and so forth. In villages and towns, it usually involves a night at an inn. All HP and AP are fully restored. It's not possible to long rest inside certain areas, such as a fortress controlled by the enemy, or a hostile wilderness or dungeon.
  • Short Rest: The party sits down for an hour or so to dress their wounds and catch their breath. Each player recovers one third of their missing HP and AP. The party can short rest at most twice between long rests.

Traveling

When traveling long distance overland, there are a number of concerns: food, water, bandits, weather, and road quality. Of these, food and bandits are the most troublesome.

Food

The basic unit of food is the day's ration. Rations are presumed to consist of nonperishable supplies such as salted meat, pickled vegetables, or hardtack. Each adventurer consumes one ration per day. When rations run out, the party is either forced to hunt, which halves travel speed, or begins starvation.

Starvation has the following effects:

  • After day 3, the party can no longer recover during short rests.
  • After day 5, the effect of a long rest is reduced to that of a short rest.
  • After day 7, the party can no longer take rests. Skill checks are made with disadvantage.
  • Starting on the 14th day, all attributes except speed are reduced by 1 each day. When strength reaches zero, the character perishes of hunger.

The effects of starvation linger even after consuming rations. In the wilderness, characters recover 1 day of hunger every day they consume rations. In villages and towns, 3 days of hunger are recovered per day. It takes approximately one week to fully recover from the brink of starvation.

Bandits

Bandits usually set up ambushes along minor trade routes and between cities, but shy away from the cities themselves and heavily travelled routes. Similarly, pirates roam the seas usually along trade routes. Naturally, all forms of banditry increase during wartime. Bandits and pirates behave differently depending on their class. Yokels and simpleton bandits usually kill their prey outright and loot their pockets. Professional bandits usually investigate their victims and attempt to discern if there is any chance of ransom. Pirates tend to sell their victims as slaves in foreign markets, while privateers tend to seek ransom.

There are no hard and fast rules for bandit encounters, but a rough understanding of their methods and techniques lends historical accuracy to the campaign.

Swimming

Whether above or under the water, movement speed is reduced by one half when swimming. So, a character with a speed score of 10 that normally moves at 5 ft/s would swim at 2.5 ft/s. It's possible to train swimming speed (Athletics Training: Freestyle), to bring swimming up to walking speed.

Underwater, players can normally hold their breath for 10 seconds per point invested in the strength attribute, after which point they must pass a DT 10 willpower saving throw every ten seconds to avoid drowning. As with swimming speed, this can be improved with training (Athletics Training: Lung Capacity). The same rules apply when holding one's breath for other reasons, such as trying to avoid inhaling toxic fumes.

Sneaking

Sneaking is handled as an ability check. The DT is 5 under near-ideal conditions (night, light background noise, not actively sought.) However, the following conditions affect the DT:

  • Dim Lighting: +5 to the DT
  • Bright Lighting: +10 to the DT
  • Absolute Silence: +3 to the DT
  • Heavy background noise: -3 to the DT
  • Obscuring fog or smoke, or partial cover (such as a crowd): -5 to the DT
  • Actively sought: The seeker's willpower ASM and perception proficiency are added to the DT.

As always, the DM may make additional adjustments as necessary.

Pickpocket

Pickpocketing is a risky but potentially rewarding endeavor. To pickpocket, you roll for sleight-of-hand, while your mark (controlled by the DM) rolls for perception. If your roll is higher than the mark's role, you successfully pick their pocket. Otherwise, they catch you with your hand in their pocket and act accordingly.

There are special rules in certain occasions:

  • If the mark is in the middle of a crowd, engaged in deep conversation, or similarly distracted they have disadvantage on the perception roll.
  • If the mark has their hands in their pockets, they have advantage on the perception roll.
  • If you are intoxicated or one of your hands is restrained, you have disadvantage on the sleight of hand roll.
  • If the city or location has a criminal reputation, the DT of the sleight of hand check is increased by 1d6.

When pickpocketing, different types of citizens usually have different amounts of money:

Lockpicking

Lockpicking is fairly straightforward. To pick a lock, you must have at least one lockpick in your inventory. You the roll a sleight-of-hand check against the DT of the lock. If successful, the lock is opened. If it fails, the lockpick is lost, but you may continue attempting to pick the lock if you have additional lockpicks.

Some special rules apply:

  • If a guard is watching, you may have to roll for stealth while picking the lock. If you fail the stealth roll, you can continue picking the lock, but will have to deal with the consequences as the guard will notice your lawless behavior.
  • If a lock is of an unusual make, the pick can break inside the lock rendering the lock unpickable. The DM should notify the player beforehand if this is likely to occur. The lockpick breaking inside the lock may also happen on a critical failure.
  • Some locks may require two picks to be used at a time.

Haggling

Players can attempt to haggle merchants using the persuasion skill. A merchant can be haggled up to three times, offering successively larger discounts if the player is able to pass successively higher DT persuasion checks. If the player fails any haggle, the merchant becomes obstinate and the discount for that item cannot be increased further. If the player fails to surpass DT 10 on any haggle, the merchant becomes irritated and removes all discounts completely.

After attempting to haggle a merchant, the same merchant will not allow themselves to be haggled again for some time, unless they are fond of the adventuring party or from a culture where haggling is expected. The DM may impose situational bonuses or penalties to haggling accordingly.
